Software craftsmanship is about being a professional:
taking responsibility for everything we do,
being passionate about what we do, it's not just a job
taking care about applications, not merely delivering them,
constantly learning to be able to solve problems using best techniques and tools (katas, pet projects, books, meetups, XP),
teaching others, helping them become better developers,
we're being perceived as experts and we should behave like ones (by saying "no" if necessary),
not only code, but also business collaboration, DevOps, testing, documentation, good estimations, interviews, driving topics, /etc.,
being proud of your work.

The focus is always on skill and quality
Some history
Craftsmanship originates from mid XIV century
Craftsmen joined together to form craft guilds
You couldn't do your craft, unless you were a member of a guild
To become a member of a guild, one had to go through 3 stages:
apprentice - lasted 5-9 years, not paid
journeyman - learnt his skill, being paid, but not master yet
master - presented his "masterpiece" built in free time and was accepted
Software craftsmanship manifesto
It's an extension to agile manifesto
Agile: Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
Craftsmen: Not only individuals and interactions, but also a community of professionals
Meetups, software conferences, go out, learn and teach, discuss
Agile: Working software over comprehensive documentation
Craftsmen: Not only working software, but also well-crafted software
Create maintainable software, something that we will be able easily extend and change in the future.
Remember: always code as if the person who ends up maintaining your code is a violent psychopath who knows where you live.
Agile: Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
Craftsmen: Not only customer collaboration, but also productive partnerships
Propose something on our own, like simulation environment for Alex regaring parcel routing
Agile: Responding to change over following a plan
Craftsmen: Not only responding to change, but also steadily adding value

Why all that?
Some obvious truths:
In the whole software lifecycle most costs goes to maintenance, not development. In total it's cheaper to spend more time and money in the beginning, but do things right.
Taking shortcuts makes us slower in the long run. Design system properly, keep quality high and any change will be fast and easy to do.
